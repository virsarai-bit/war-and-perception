When America
Goes to War

How U.S. military conflicts in Muslim-majority countries have shaped the way Americans see Muslim people at home — from World War II to the present.

Read the Argument ↓

Between 1939 and today, the United States has engaged in military operations across dozens of predominantly Muslim countries — from Saudi Arabia and Iran to Afghanistan, Iraq, Somalia, Libya, and beyond. These conflicts have been justified, debated, and eventually archived. But their effects don't end at the border. This project asks a harder question: what do these wars do to the people left at home?

Specifically — what do they do to the image of Muslims in the American imagination? Through polling data, government policy, and media representation, a pattern emerges that is difficult to dismiss: war abroad has consistently made life harder for Muslims in America. This project traces that pattern, names it, and explains why it persists.

01

The Argument

The Central Claim

U.S. military involvement in Muslim-majority countries has directly and measurably worsened domestic perceptions of Muslims. This is not simply the result of isolated events like the September 11 attacks. It is a structural pattern — one traceable through decades of polling data, government policy decisions, and the way news and entertainment media have portrayed Muslim people.

Each time the United States has entered a new conflict in a Muslim-majority country, Americans' attitudes toward Muslims at home have shifted. Approval ratings drop. Discrimination reports spike. Policy becomes more restrictive. And in media, stereotypes that were already present become more prominent, more widespread, and more acceptable in public discourse.

"The relationship between foreign policy and domestic prejudice is not coincidental. It is structural — built into how the United States imagines the world it chooses to fight in."

This is not a claim that American soldiers or policymakers intend to harm Muslim Americans. It is a claim about systems: about how the language used to justify military action abroad filters back into how ordinary Americans perceive the people who share those cultural or religious identities at home. The mechanism is partly media, partly political rhetoric, and partly a worldview that has deep historical roots — one that scholars call Orientalism.

The Framework: Orientalism

To understand why this pattern exists — not just that it does — this project uses a theoretical lens developed by scholar Edward Said in his 1978 book, Orientalism.

Said's argument, stated plainly, is this: the West — Europe and the United States — has historically constructed an image of the "East" (the Arab world, the Muslim world, the "Orient") not as it actually is, but as the West needs it to be. This imagined Orient is portrayed as exotic, irrational, dangerous, and backward. Crucially, this image is not a reflection of reality. It is a projection — one that serves a political purpose: it justifies Western military dominance, colonialism, and "civilizing" interventions by making the dominated people appear to need it.

In plain terms: Orientalism is the practice of defining a people as fundamentally "other" — as inferior, threatening, or incomprehensible — in order to justify controlling or dominating them. When the United States describes Muslim-majority countries as "uncivilized," "terrorist," or "in need of liberation," it is using Orientalist language. And that same language doesn't stay in foreign policy briefings. It seeps into news coverage, movies, political speeches — and eventually into how ordinary Americans view their Muslim neighbors.

Said's framework was originally developed to analyze how European literary and academic texts represented the Arab world. But scholars like Deepa Kumar (2012) and Douglas Little (2004) have extended it specifically to U.S. foreign policy and the War on Terror. Their work shows that Orientalist assumptions don't just describe U.S. policy — they enable it, by constructing a public imagination in which Muslim people are a category of threat rather than a community of individuals.

This project builds on their work to ask one further question: if Orientalist thinking shapes how America portrays and justifies its wars, does that same thinking shape how America treats Muslim Americans? The evidence strongly suggests: yes.

02

The Evidence

The argument rests on three categories of evidence: what Americans believe (polling data), what the government has done (policy), and what the culture has produced (media representation). Together, they form a mutually reinforcing picture.

Polling Data

What Americans Think — and Have Always Thought

Public opinion data on American attitudes toward Muslims has been collected systematically for over three decades. The picture it paints is consistent, and troubling — and it challenges the popular narrative that American hostility toward Muslims is simply a reaction to terrorism.

The Bridge Initiative's Super Survey — a synthesis of over 400 surveys conducted between 1993 and 2014 — found that Americans consistently rate Islam as more violent than other major religions. Critically, these ratings spike during periods of active U.S. military engagement in Muslim-majority countries. The correlation is not subtle, and it does not point only toward major terrorist attacks as the driver.

The Pew Research Center's 2017 survey of Muslim Americans adds the human dimension to these numbers. Half of all Muslim Americans — citizens and permanent residents who work, vote, and pay taxes — reported facing discrimination in the single year before the survey. More than half said their daily experience of being Muslim in America had become harder since the September 11 attacks — attacks that no Muslim American committed, and that the overwhelming majority of Muslim Americans publicly condemned.

"The data does not simply show a rise in fear after terrorist attacks. It shows a rise in fear that tracks U.S. military activity overseas — beginning in the early 1990s with operations in Somalia, Bosnia, and the Gulf, and never returning to pre-engagement levels."
— Interpretation based on Bridge Initiative Super Survey analysis

This distinction matters enormously. The dominant narrative — that American hostility toward Muslims is a rational response to terrorism — is not supported by the longitudinal data. Negative attitudes were rising before most major attacks and have remained elevated in years without any attacks on U.S. soil. What the data correlates with is not attack frequency, but the presence of U.S. forces in Muslim-majority countries. War shapes perception before the bodies come home.

Government Policy

When Perception Becomes Law

Public perception does not stay in the realm of opinion — it eventually shapes policy. And when that perception is shaped by militarized fear of a religious group, the policy that results can look a great deal like institutionalized discrimination.

No single document illustrates this more clearly than Executive Order 13769, signed by President Donald Trump on January 27, 2017.

Primary Document

Executive Order 13769

"Protecting the Nation From Foreign Terrorist Entry Into the United States" — Federal Register 82, no. 20 (2017): 8977–8982

The order banned citizens of seven Muslim-majority countries — Iran, Iraq, Libya, Somalia, Sudan, Syria, and Yemen — from entering the United States for 90 days, suspended the U.S. refugee program for 120 days, and indefinitely banned Syrian refugees. It explicitly invoked the September 11 attacks as justification — despite the fact that none of the 9/11 hijackers came from any of the seven countries listed in the order.

Federal judges who blocked portions of the order noted that all seven countries listed were either active sites of U.S. military operations or countries that had experienced recent, significant U.S. military involvement. The order was, in effect, a translation of military targeting into immigration law: if the U.S. military operated there, its citizens were assumed dangerous.

The order was quickly labeled the "Muslim Ban" by its critics and, informally, by some of its supporters. Regardless of legal framing, its practical effect was to institutionalize the idea that origin from a Muslim-majority country was, in itself, a security risk — a direct expression of the Orientalist logic Said described four decades earlier: the "Muslim world" treated as a single, undifferentiated category of threat.

E.O. 13769 was not an aberration. It was the most visible expression of a policy current that had been building since the early days of the War on Terror: the PATRIOT Act's surveillance provisions disproportionately targeting Muslim communities, the expansion of the No-Fly List, the NSA's mass data collection programs, and documented FBI infiltration of mosques and Muslim student organizations. Each of these represents the same logic — military conflict abroad justifies surveillance and restriction at home — applied to a religious community that the government had come to treat as an extension of its foreign enemies.

Media Representation

The Stories America Tells Itself

Policy is visible. Statistics are legible. But some of the most powerful forces shaping perception operate beneath conscious awareness — in the movies we watch as children, the cartoons absorbed before we can read, and the news segments that frame how we understand the world outside our experience. Media representation is both a symptom of Orientalist thinking and one of its primary engines.

The clips below — drawn from documentaries, news archives, and pop culture — illustrate the range of ways Muslim people have been portrayed in American media, particularly during and after periods of military conflict. Sources

Six sources form the evidentiary and theoretical foundation of this project. Each is cited in Chicago style and annotated using the BEAM framework: Background, Exhibit, Argument, Method.

Background

Bridge Initiative. The Super Survey. Georgetown University, 2015.

This source provides the empirical baseline for the project's claims about American public opinion. By synthesizing over 400 surveys conducted between 1993 and 2014, it establishes that negative attitudes toward Muslims in the United States have been persistent, widespread, and correlated with periods of U.S. military activity in the Muslim world — not merely reactions to specific attacks on U.S. soil.

Argument

Kumar, Deepa. Islamophobia and the Politics of Empire. Haymarket Books, 2012.

Kumar's book is the project's primary argumentative source. She argues that Islamophobia is not a free-floating cultural prejudice but a structural phenomenon — produced and maintained by U.S. imperial interests. Her analysis connects anti-Muslim sentiment directly to the political economy of U.S. military engagement, providing the causal mechanism this project needs to move beyond correlation and argue for a structural relationship between war and domestic discrimination.

Argument

Little, Douglas. "Orientalism and the Middle East." The International History Review 26, no. 1 (2004): 110–135.

Little applies Said's Orientalist framework to the specific context of U.S. Cold War and post-Cold War policy in the Middle East, arguing that Orientalist assumptions were not merely cultural artifacts but active shaping forces in U.S. policy decisions. This source bridges the gap between Said's literary analysis and the political and military history this project examines — showing how intellectual frameworks become operational in government decision-making.

Method / Theory

Said, Edward W. Orientalism. Pantheon Books, 1978.

Said's foundational text provides the theoretical lens through which all other evidence in this project is interpreted. His argument — that the West constructs an imagined "Orient" defined by inferiority, irrationality, and danger, as a justification for domination rather than as a description of reality — allows this project to identify not just that anti-Muslim representation exists, but why it exists and how it functions ideologically to enable military and political control.

Exhibit

Pew Research Center. U.S. Muslims Concerned About Their Place in Society. 2017.

This survey report provides the most direct evidence of the human impact of the patterns this project documents. Analyzed here as an exhibit — a primary document revealing the lived experience of Muslim Americans through their own reported accounts — its finding that approximately half of Muslim Americans experienced discrimination in a single year, and that over half feel the post-9/11 environment has materially worsened their daily lives, grounds the project's argument in reported experience rather than inference.

Exhibit

Trump, Donald. Executive Order 13769. Federal Register 82, no. 20 (2017): 8977–8982.

The executive order itself — the primary government document — is analyzed as an exhibit showing how the militarized framing of Muslim people in U.S. foreign policy translates directly into domestic law. The order's explicit invocation of the September 11 attacks to justify restricting citizens of Muslim-majority countries that had no connection to those attacks is a textbook illustration of the Orientalist logic Said described: the "Muslim world" collapsed into a single, undifferentiated threat category, then legislated against.

About This Project

I am a first-year student at Emory University (Oxford College), studying Political Science and Marketing. This website was created as an engagement tool for a research project in REL226. It is intended not just as an academic exercise, but as a resource that might be genuinely useful to anyone trying to understand the relationship between U.S. foreign policy and the domestic experiences of Muslim Americans.

My interest in this topic comes from a broader fascination with U.S. foreign policy, public memory, and the ways in which geopolitical decisions ripple into the daily lives of communities far from the battlefield. The question this project asks — how does a government's choice to go to war shape how its own citizens treat each other — seems to me one of the most important questions that rarely gets asked in mainstream political conversation.

This site was designed to be shared freely. If you are a student, a teacher, or simply someone who found their way here through curiosity, I hope it gives you something to think about.
