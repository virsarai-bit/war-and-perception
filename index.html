When America
Goes to War

How U.S. military conflicts in Muslim-majority countries have shaped the way Americans see Muslim people at home.

Read the Argument ↓
Conflicts since 1939: World War II· Korean War· Gulf War· Somalia· Bosnia· Kosovo· Afghanistan· Iraq· Libya· Syria· Yemen
Draft intro — edit me

Between 1939 and today, the United States has engaged in military operations across dozens of predominantly Muslim countries. This project asks what those wars do to the people left at home: specifically, to the image of Muslims in the American imagination. Through polling data, government policy, and media representation, one pattern emerges. War abroad has consistently made life harder for Muslims in America.

Draft copy — edit me

U.S. military involvement in Muslim-majority countries has directly and measurably worsened domestic perceptions of Muslims. This is not simply the result of isolated events like the September 11 attacks. It is a structural pattern traceable through decades of polling data, government policy decisions, and the way news and entertainment media have portrayed Muslim people.

The Framework: Orientalism

In plain terms: Orientalism is the practice of defining a people as fundamentally "other" in order to justify controlling them. When the United States describes Muslim-majority countries as "uncivilized," "terrorist," or "in need of liberation," it is using Orientalist language. That language does not stay in foreign policy briefings. It seeps into news coverage, movies, political speeches, and eventually into how ordinary Americans view their Muslim neighbors.

Draft copy — edit me

Said's framework was originally developed to analyze how European literary and academic texts represented the Arab world. Scholars like Deepa Kumar (2012) and Douglas Little (2004) have extended it to U.S. foreign policy and the War on Terror, showing that Orientalist assumptions don't just describe U.S. policy: they enable it, by constructing a public imagination in which Muslim people are a category of threat rather than a community of individuals.

Draft copy — edit me

The Bridge Initiative's synthesis of over 400 surveys (1993–2014) found that Americans consistently rate Islam as more violent than other major religions, with spikes during periods of active U.S. military engagement. The Pew Research Center's 2017 survey adds the human dimension: half of all Muslim Americans reported facing discrimination in a single year, and more than half said their daily experience of being Muslim had become harder since September 11, for attacks that no Muslim American committed.

The critical distinction: anti-Muslim sentiment correlates with U.S. military activity, not attack frequency. Negative attitudes were rising through the mid-1990s during operations in Somalia, Bosnia, and the Gulf, and have never returned to pre-1990 levels regardless of the domestic threat environment.

Government Policy

When Perception Becomes Law

Primary Document  ·  Exhibit Source

Executive Order 13769

"Protecting the Nation From Foreign Terrorist Entry Into the United States." Federal Register 82, no. 20 (2017): 8977–8982

The order banned citizens of seven Muslim-majority countries (Iran, Iraq, Libya, Somalia, Sudan, Syria, and Yemen) from entering the United States for 90 days, suspended the refugee program for 120 days, and indefinitely banned Syrian refugees. It explicitly invoked the September 11 attacks as justification, despite the fact that none of the 9/11 hijackers came from any of the seven countries listed.

Federal judges who blocked portions of the order noted that all seven countries were either active sites of U.S. military operations or countries that had experienced significant U.S. military involvement. In effect: a translation of military targeting into immigration law.

Draft copy — edit me

E.O. 13769 was quickly labeled the "Muslim Ban." Regardless of legal framing, its practical effect was to institutionalize the idea that origin from a Muslim-majority country was, in itself, a security risk: a direct expression of the Orientalist logic Said described four decades earlier. It was the most visible expression of a policy current that had been building since the early days of the War on Terror, including the PATRIOT Act's surveillance provisions, the expansion of the No-Fly List, and documented FBI infiltration of mosques and Muslim student organizations.

Sources

Six sources form the evidentiary and theoretical foundation of this project. Each is cited in Chicago style and annotated using the BEAM framework: Background, Exhibit, Argument, Method.

Background

Bridge Initiative. The Super Survey. Georgetown University, 2015.

This source provides the empirical baseline for the project's claims about American public opinion. By synthesizing over 400 surveys conducted between 1993 and 2014, it establishes that negative attitudes toward Muslims in the United States have been persistent, widespread, and correlated with periods of U.S. military activity in the Muslim world, not merely reactions to specific attacks on U.S. soil.

Argument

Kumar, Deepa. Islamophobia and the Politics of Empire. Haymarket Books, 2012.

Kumar's book is the project's primary argumentative source. She argues that Islamophobia is not a free-floating cultural prejudice but a structural phenomenon, produced and maintained by U.S. imperial interests. Her analysis connects anti-Muslim sentiment directly to the political economy of U.S. military engagement, providing the causal mechanism this project needs to move beyond correlation and toward a structural account of how war and domestic discrimination are linked.

Argument

Little, Douglas. "Orientalism and the Middle East." The International History Review 26, no. 1 (2004): 110–135.

Little applies Said's Orientalist framework to the specific context of U.S. Cold War and post-Cold War policy in the Middle East, arguing that Orientalist assumptions were not merely cultural artifacts but active shaping forces in U.S. policy decisions. This source bridges the gap between Said's literary analysis and the political and military history this project examines, showing how intellectual frameworks become operational in government decision-making.

Method / Theory

Said, Edward W. Orientalism. Pantheon Books, 1978.

Said's foundational text provides the theoretical lens through which all other evidence in this project is interpreted. His argument that the West constructs an imagined "Orient" defined by inferiority, irrationality, and danger as a justification for domination (rather than as a description of reality) allows this project to identify not just that anti-Muslim representation exists, but why it exists and how it functions ideologically to enable military and political control.

Exhibit

Pew Research Center. U.S. Muslims Concerned About Their Place in Society. 2017.

This survey report provides the most direct evidence of the human impact of the patterns this project documents. Analyzed as an exhibit, its finding that approximately half of Muslim Americans experienced discrimination in a single year, and that over half feel the post-9/11 environment has materially worsened their daily lives, grounds the project's argument in reported experience rather than inference.

Exhibit

Trump, Donald. Executive Order 13769. Federal Register 82, no. 20 (2017): 8977–8982.

The executive order itself is analyzed as an exhibit showing how the militarized framing of Muslim people in U.S. foreign policy translates directly into domestic law. The order's explicit invocation of the September 11 attacks to justify restricting citizens of Muslim-majority countries that had no connection to those attacks is a textbook illustration of the Orientalist logic Said described: the "Muslim world" collapsed into a single, undifferentiated threat category, then legislated against.

About This Project

I am a first-year student at Emory University (Oxford College), studying Political Science and Marketing. This website was created as an engagement tool for a research project in REL226. It is intended not just as an academic exercise, but as a resource that might be genuinely useful to anyone trying to understand the relationship between U.S. foreign policy and the domestic experiences of Muslim Americans.

My interest in this topic comes from a broader fascination with U.S. foreign policy, public memory, and the ways in which geopolitical decisions ripple into the daily lives of communities far from the battlefield. The question this project asks (how does a government's choice to go to war shape how its own citizens treat each other) seems to me one of the most important questions that rarely gets asked in mainstream political conversation.

This site was designed to be shared freely. If you are a student, a teacher, or simply someone who found their way here through curiosity, I hope it gives you something to think about.
